Market-standard gaps, missing coverage, and excessive terms

1. Flood — universal gap across the entire portfolio

Not one of the 9 quotes/declarations reviewed (across all 3 properties, current and prospective) includes flood coverage. This is standard practice in Florida — flood is written separately via NFIP or a private flood market — but it means zero of your properties currently carry flood insurance despite being in Central Florida flood-zone-adjacent areas (Orange/Seminole County). Worth a deliberate decision rather than a default: get flood quotes for at least Cider Mill and Birgham (older homes, historically more exposed) even if Asher is judged low-risk enough to skip.

2. Water damage — the biggest real differentiator, and it's inconsistent within each property

For Asher alone, water-damage terms range from fully covered with no sublimit (Frontline, CORE) to capped at $10,000 (Edison) to capped at $25,000 (Loggerhead, Florida Peninsula) — a 100%-to-0% swing in protection for what is consistently the #1 homeowners claim type in Florida (non-hurricane water damage). The same pattern repeats at Cider Mill (Orange/Security First cap at $10k, Florida Peninsula at $25k) and is worst at Birgham, where Geovera excludes water damage entirely. Carriers offer a small premium credit (roughly $150–$600/yr across these quotes) for accepting the lower sublimit — that's the actual price of the gap, and it's worth pricing out explicitly rather than defaulting to whichever carrier is cheapest overall.

3. Sinkhole coverage — present on exactly one quote

CORE is the only Asher quote (and only quote across all 3 properties) that includes sinkhole coverage (10% deductible, full policy limit). Every other carrier — Edison, Frontline, Loggerhead, Florida Peninsula, and both Cider Mill quotes — explicitly excludes it. Central Florida (Orange/Seminole counties) has documented sinkhole activity; standard Florida HO3/DP3 policies only cover "catastrophic ground cover collapse" (home condemned/uninhabitable) by default, which is a much narrower trigger than full sinkhole coverage. This is a conscious risk decision, not a rounding error — worth deciding property-by-property rather than letting it fall out by which carrier happens to be cheapest.

4. Liability limits — uniformly $300k, no umbrella anywhere

Every single quote across every property caps personal/premises liability at exactly $300,000 (Citizens excludes it entirely for the corporate-owned properties). None of the 8 agents quoting this round have proposed or mentioned an umbrella/excess liability policy. You own 3 properties (one primary, two rentals with tenants), which aggregates liability exposure in a way a single $300k-per-policy limit doesn't address — a slip-and-fall or dog-bite claim at any one property is capped at $300k regardless of your total net worth or portfolio size. A $1M umbrella policy is typically inexpensive (a few hundred dollars a year) relative to the exposure it removes, and is standard practice for landlords with multiple properties. Worth asking one of your agents (Rigetta or Sihle, since they're closest to binding) to quote one.

5. Excessive personal property assumptions — Frontline and Florida Peninsula (Asher)

Edison, CORE, and Loggerhead all price Asher's Personal Property (Coverage C) at ~25% of the dwelling limit ($99,750–$109,250) — consistent with each other. Frontline and Florida Peninsula both price it at ~50% ($203,548 and $215,850) — roughly double. Frontline's Loss of Use is similarly doubled (20% vs. the 10% every other carrier uses). This isn't a coverage upgrade you asked for — it looks like these two carriers defaulted to the generic ISO 50%-of-dwelling contents assumption rather than a value tailored to your actual furnishings. That inflated base directly drives Florida Peninsula's premium to $6,014.83 — the most expensive Asher quote by over $1,500/yr — and adds real (if smaller) cost to Frontline's $4,265.18. Ask both agents whether Contents/Loss of Use can be dialed down to match the other quotes (~25%/10%); if Florida Peninsula's premium drops proportionally, it becomes far more competitive with everything else on the table.

6. Personal Property Replacement Cost — bundled free on some, a paid add-on on others

Edison, CORE, and Orange all include full replacement-cost valuation on contents at no extra charge ("Included"). Loggerhead charges $178.82 for it and Florida Peninsula charges $665.71 — both list it as a separate line-item premium rather than a bundled feature. Same coverage, real price difference buried in the fine print — worth factoring into any head-to-head premium comparison rather than comparing bottom-line totals alone.

7. Florida Peninsula's Cider Mill quote caps roof payout at a stated value, not full replacement

The Florida Peninsula DP3 quote for Cider Mill lists a "Stated Value Roof Limit" of $11,600 — a separate, capped payout basis for roof losses distinct from the $390,900 Coverage A dwelling limit. Orange's competing quote for the same property has no such limitation. A full roof replacement after a covered loss commonly runs well above $11,600 for a house this size — if a storm damages the roof, Florida Peninsula's payout may be capped at that stated amount with the owner/the LLC covering the shortfall, whichever policy it's under. This is the single most consequential fine-print difference found between competing quotes on the same property and should be confirmed with the agent before considering this option, regardless of its otherwise-competitive premium.

8. Reciprocal exchange "surplus contributions" — a structural cost most people don't compare

Frontline, Loggerhead, CORE, and Orange are all reciprocal exchanges, and each tacks on a "Member's Surplus Contribution" (roughly 9–10% of base premium: $382 on Frontline, $350.71 on Loggerhead, $266.20 on Orange, folded into CORE's combined fee line) on top of the quoted coverage premium. Edison and Florida Peninsula are traditional stock companies with no equivalent line. This isn't a red flag — reciprocal exchanges are a normal, regulated Florida market structure — but it means their "base premium" understates the real cost relative to a stock-company quote by design, and the total premium (which already accounts for it) is the only fair number to compare.

9. Credit-based insurance scoring is quietly costing money on at least two quotes

Both Edison and Florida Peninsula's Asher quotes include an explicit disclosure that the owner did not receive the lowest possible rate due to credit-based insurance score factors — specifically, accounts with 30-day late payments reported and insufficient information on certain account types (department store / personal finance tradelines). Since Florida insurers broadly use credit-based insurance scoring, this same drag likely applies across every carrier quoting Asher, not just these two. Pulling and correcting the underlying credit report (both carriers cite the same LexisNexis-sourced reason codes) is a one-time fix that could lower every future renewal, not just this round's quotes.

10. Minor/optional gaps present across nearly every quote (low materiality, worth a mention)

  • Equipment breakdown (AC/appliance mechanical failure) — "No coverage" or unselected on every quote that lists it (Edison, Frontline, Florida Peninsula). Inexpensive endorsement, commonly bundled elsewhere.
  • Identity theft — absent on most; Florida Peninsula offers it on Asher for $25/yr but it's not available at all on the landlord (DP3) policies, which is normal since tenants, not the owner, occupy those homes.
  • Animal liability — universally excluded or "not available" across every quote reviewed.
  • Screened enclosure coverage — included by default on Florida Peninsula's Asher quote, but not clearly itemized anywhere for Birgham despite the property having a 500 sq ft screened pool enclosure per the Geovera prefill report (see section 3 above, Birgham) — worth confirming explicitly rather than assuming it falls under Other Structures.

11. GreatFlorida's cheapest-on-every-property pattern: the savings are real, but so are the exclusions

A newly-discovered agency, GreatFlorida Insurance of Ocoee, returned the single cheapest quote on all 3 properties — each through a different carrier, and each cheaper by $1,000–$1,600/yr than the next-best option. That's a meaningful pattern worth naming directly: on every property, the cheapest quote is the one giving up the most coverage.
  • Mangrove (Asher, $2,375): lowest dwelling limit of any Asher quote ($349k vs. $399k–$437k elsewhere) and sinkhole wasn't selected.
  • Tower Hill (Cider Mill, $1,988): two confirmed exclusion endorsements — "Sinkhole Exclusion: Included" and "Water Damage Exclusion: Included." This is the only Cider Mill quote with zero water damage coverage, worse than every other option's $10k–$25k sublimit.
  • American Integrity (Birgham, $1,715): a DP1 "Dwelling Basic" policy — a named-perils form, structurally narrower than every other quote's DP3/HO3 "special form" coverage — and it excludes personal property entirely.
This doesn't mean these quotes are bad — American Integrity in particular solves the Other-Structures underinsurance problem at Birgham better than anyone else. But price comparisons across this whole project should weight coverage breadth as heavily as premium, and GreatFlorida's numbers are the clearest illustration of why.
